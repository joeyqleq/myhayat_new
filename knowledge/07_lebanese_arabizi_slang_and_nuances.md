# Comprehensive Lebanese Arabizi and Colloquial Slang Dictionary

## 1. Introduction to Lebanese Arabizi
Arabizi (Arabic + English) is the informal, digital written form of the Levantine Arabic dialect, primarily used by Lebanese youth across messaging apps and social media platforms like Reddit. Because the Latin alphabet lacks letters to represent certain Arabic phonetic sounds, numbers are used as visual or phonetic substitutes. 

### The Arabizi Alphabet Key
*   **2** = ء (Hamza) - A glottal stop. Example: *t2tlne* (kill me), *la2* (no), *sa2eel* (annoying).
*   **3** = ع (Ayn) - A deep pharyngeal sound. Example: *3alam* (people), *3al* (great), *bya3erfo* (they know), *3eeshi* (life/living).
*   **5** = خ (Khaa) - A raspy 'kh' sound (like 'Bach'). Example: *5abrak* (tell you), *sha5s* (person).
*   **7** = ح (Haa) - A sharp, breathy 'h'. Example: *7abibi* (darling), *7al* (solution), *7alon* (themselves).
*   **8** = غ (Ghayn) - A gargling 'gh' sound. Example: *8ayran* (jealous), *bta8let* (you make a mistake).
*   **9** = ص (Saad) - A heavy 's'. Example: *9a7* (correct).

## 2. Common Conversational Fillers & Address
Lebanese discourse is heavily reliant on terms of endearment, even when the tone of the conversation is aggressive, sarcastic, or exasperated.

*   **Ya khaye / Ya bro:** "My brother" / "Bro". Used to express camaraderie, frustration, or a plea for understanding. (e.g., *ktir disrespectful ya bro*).
*   **Ya habibi:** Literally "my darling," but in political or heated arguments, it is often used condescendingly or sarcastically to mean "my naive friend."
*   **Yo2borne / Yo2borne ana:** Literally "may he/she bury me" (meaning I love them so much I want to die before them so I don't have to live without them). Can be used sarcastically to mock someone acting overly innocent. (e.g., *yo2borne ana el hezb maleyek nezlin mnel sama* - "oh bury me they are angels descending from the sky").
*   **Wahyetak:** "I swear by your life." Used to emphasize the absolute truth of a statement.
*   **Ana azde:** "I mean..." or "My intention is..."
*   **Tayeb 3al lakan:** "Okay great, then..." (Often used to passively accept a terrible situation or sarcastically agree).
*   **Istg:** An English abbreviation for "I swear to God," frequently mixed directly into Arabizi sentences.

## 3. Expressions of Severe Frustration & Anger
Decades of war, sectarian conflict, and economic collapse have introduced severe, fatalistic profanity into everyday venting. These are not necessarily directed at the listener, but at the system, the country, or the general situation.

*   **Kis ekht hal 3eeshi:** Literally "The sister's v**** of this life." A profound expression of misery and exhaustion with living conditions.
*   **Khara 3lek / Khara 3a hal dawle:** "Sh*t on you" / "Sh*t on this state (government)." 
*   **Ayre b...:** "My d*** in..." Used to express ultimate disdain for a concept, group, or situation. 
*   **A5o sharmouta:** "Son of a wh*re." Can be used aggressively or even descriptively for a terrible situation.
*   **Mish na2isna naseb w ser2a:** "We aren't missing any more scams and theft" (We have had enough of being robbed by the government/factions).

## 4. Emotional & Political Fatigue Idioms
*   **Bele3 bri2i:** Literally "swallowing my saliva." Used to express that someone has been struggling, waiting in suspense, or enduring hardship for a long time without relief.
*   **Nehna baad ma shefna she:** "We haven't seen anything yet." Reflects the constant Lebanese anxiety that no matter how bad things are, worse is coming.
*   **Ma elish jledet sama rabkoun:** "I don't have the energy/patience for your God's sky." A heavy expression of complete mental exhaustion, meaning "I have zero patience left to deal with your nonsense."
*   **Sho feena na3mal:** "What can we do?" The ultimate phrase of Lebanese resignation and helplessness.

## 5. Code-Switching & Hybrid Syntax
Lebanese youth speak a tri-lingual hybrid of Arabic, English, and French. English phrases are frequently conjugated using Arabic grammar, and sentences often start in one language and end in another.

*   *Example:* "Bro wahyetak that’s not what I’m trying to say, ana azde men sanctions to internal support la kel hol." (Bro I swear that's not what I'm trying to say, I mean from sanctions to internal support for all of these).
*   *Example:* "i banned him 5 days... report him in private shu hal post ma elo aaze." (I banned him 5 days... report him in private what is this post it has no use).
*   *Example:* "Nshallah kher, the country needs it." (God willing it's good, the country needs it).

## 6. The Function of Dark Humor in Trauma
The Lebanese cope with severe, repetitive trauma (war, explosions, economic ruin) through intense dark humor. 
*   **The Tears of Joy Emoji (😂/😭):** These emojis are frequently paired with statements about death, war, poverty, or sectarian violence. 
*   *Example:* "Ya habibi sho bade bl internet eza fe 3alam bada teje t2tlne l2an ana men tayfe m3ayne 😂" (My friend, what do I want with the internet if there are people who want to come kill me because I'm from a specific sect 😂).
*   *Example:* "Give it 5 more minutes until Israel b7arkish bl jnoub again 😭" (Give it 5 more minutes until Israel pokes at the south again 😭).

In the Lebanese context, these emojis do not represent actual joy or lightheartedness. They are a psychological shield—a trauma response used to mask deep-seated fear and helplessness in the face of uncontrollable systemic violence and corruption.
